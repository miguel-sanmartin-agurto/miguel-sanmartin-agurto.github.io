## BESS System sizing

Consider the preliminary sizing of a Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) connected at 33 kV / 50 Hz.

# Project requirements

- Power at the point of interconnection (POI): 50 MW  
- Duration: 4 hours  
- Grid frequency: 50 Hz  
- Power factor: 0.9  
- Interconnection voltage: 33 kV  
- DC cable losses: 0.2%  
- Medium-voltage AC cable losses: 0.5%  
- The point of interconnection (POI) is located at the 33 kV busbar of the collector substation  

![Simplified system diagram](/blog/2026_04_26/Simplified_System_Diagram.jpg)

In order to clarify the project calculations, the single-line diagram is divided into four reference points:

- 1: Point of interconnection (POI)  
- 2: CSI MV SKID output  
- 3: CSI MV SKID input  
- 4: Battery output  

The section between points 2 and 1 corresponds to the AC wiring. Similarly, the section between points 3 and 4 corresponds to DC wiring.

In this report, these numbers will be used as subscripts to indicate the point at which power or energy is being calculated.

---

## Power sizing

# Minimum active power

To calculate the minimum active power that must be provided by the inverter (CSI MV SKID), the losses in the AC cabling must be considered:

$$
P_2 = \frac{P_1}{(1 - L_{AC})}
$$

Where:

- $P_i$: Power at point *i* [MW], i = (1,2)  
- $L_{AC}$: AC wiring losses (0.5%)  

Substituting values:

$$
P_2 = \frac{50}{(1 - 0.005)} = 50.25 \text{ MW}
$$

Similarly, considering inverter efficiency (98%) and DC losses (0.2%), the results are:

| Point | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|:------|:----:|:----:|:----:|:----:|
| Power [MW] | 50 | 50.25 | 51.28 | 51.38 |

An important aspect is to verify that the inverter is capable of supplying the required apparent power.

The power factor is defined as:

$$
FP = \frac{P}{S}
$$

Rearranging:

$$
S = \frac{P}{FP} = \frac{50.25}{0.9} = 55.83 \text{ MVA}
$$

# Assumptions

For this preliminary sizing, it is assumed that the losses of 0.5% (AC) and 0.2% (DC) include both Joule losses and voltage drop.

In a detailed engineering stage, conductor sizing and routing distances must be calculated to ensure compliance with voltage drop limits and cost optimization.

## Energy sizing

# Minimum required energy on DC side

The required energy is calculated similarly to power, multiplied by the backup time:

$$
E_4 = \frac{E_1}{(1 - L_{DC})(1 - L_{AC}) \eta_{CSI}}
$$

Where:

- $E_i$: Energy at point *i* [MWh], i = (1,4)  
- $L_{AC}$: AC losses (0.5%)  
- $L_{DC}$: DC losses (0.2%)  
- $\eta_{CSI}$: Inverter efficiency (98%)  

Energy at POI:

$$
E_1 = P_1 \cdot t_{backup}
$$

Substituting:

$$
E_4 = \frac{50 \cdot 4}{(1 - 0.002)(1 - 0.005)\cdot 0.98} = 205.52 \text{ MWh}
$$

# Assumptions

A Beginning of Life (BOL) scenario is considered.

In real design, degradation (SoH) must be considered to ensure required energy at End of Life (EOL), typically via overbuild.

---

## System Architecture and Equipment Quantity

# Number of PCS units

$$
PCS_{Qty} = \frac{S}{P_{CSI}}
$$

Where:

- $PCS_{Qty}$: Number of inverters  
- $S$: Required apparent power [MVA]  
- $P_{CSI}$: Nominal inverter power [MVA]  

Given:

$$
PCS_{Qty} = \frac{55.83}{5.16} = 10.82
$$

Minimum required: **11 inverters**

---

# Number of battery containers

$$
Bat_{Qty} = \frac{E_4}{E_{bat}}
$$

Where:

- $Bat_{Qty}$: Number of battery containers  
- $E_4$: Required energy [MWh]  
- $E_{bat}$: Battery usable energy [MWh]  

$$
Bat_{Qty} = \frac{205.52}{4.8} = 42.81
$$

Minimum required: **43 battery containers**

---

## Block architecture

$$
P_{PCS} > n \cdot Bat_{group}
$$

Max batteries per inverter:

$$
\frac{5160}{1200} = 4.3 \Rightarrow 4 \text{ batteries per inverter}
$$

# Compatibility check

| Requirements | Inverter | Battery group |
|:-------------|:----------:|:--------------:|
| Nominal power [MW] | 5.16 | 4.8 |
| DC voltage [V] | 1000–1500 | 1164.8 – 1497.6 |
| Max short circuit [kA] | 20 | 10 |

# Proposed configurations

- **Option 1 (Asymmetric):**  
  10 blocks (1 inverter + 4 batteries) + 1 block (1 inverter + 3 batteries)

- **Option 2 (Symmetric):**  
  11 blocks (1 inverter + 4 batteries) → 44 batteries total  

This option improves resilience, simplifies control (PPC), and provides energy margin for degradation.
